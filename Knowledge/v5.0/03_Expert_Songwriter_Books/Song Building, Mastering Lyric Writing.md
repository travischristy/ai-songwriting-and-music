**III. Core Methodology: Song Building and Blueprinting**

This is the central pillar of Dotson's teaching.

1. **Song Building:** The overarching philosophy of treating songwriting as a constructive process, akin to building a house. It involves elements like structures, blueprinting, framing, furniture, and finishing touches.
2. **Song Blueprinting:**
    - **Definition:** A clear outline or map of the song *before* writing lyrics, boiling each section down to one simple idea (a single sentence or statement).
    - **Purpose/Benefits:**
        - Ensures clarity and avoids getting stuck (especially in Verse 2).
        - Saves time, particularly in co-writing by aligning collaborators.
        - Facilitates clear communication with the listener.
        - Helps determine if a song idea has enough potential.
    - **Analogy:** Building a house without a blueprint leads to errors and omissions. Bill O'Hanlon's book-writing experience (3 years for first book vs. months for later ones using blueprinting) is cited.
    - **Process:** Focus on blueprinting two verses, the chorus, and the bridge (if used). The pre-chorus is usually part of the verse idea.
    - **Key Principle:** The blueprint should define *what* you want to communicate in each section, not *how* you'll say it, thus opening up creative possibilities rather than limiting them. Good blueprints avoid excessive complexity (e.g., multiple ideas connected by "ands" or commas in a section's summary).

**Chapter 1: Song Structure and Blueprinting**

- Reiterates blueprinting as the crucial first step.
- **Modern Commercial Song Structures:**
    - **Form 1:** Verse - Pre-Chorus (Channel/Lift) - Chorus - Verse - Pre-Chorus (optional in V2) - Chorus - Bridge (Middle Eight) - Chorus.
    - **Form 2:** Verse - Chorus - Verse - Chorus - Bridge - Chorus.
    - Notes exceptions (no bridge, chorus repeats, chorus start as production choices). Mentions refrains as an alternative to choruses but less common in modern commercial songs.
- **Simplified Blueprinting Form:** Verse 1 (± Pre-Chorus), Chorus, Verse 2 (± Pre-Chorus), Bridge (only if needed).
- **Purpose of Each Song Section:**
    - **Verse 1:** Introduce character, context, setting (who, to whom, where, what's happening, relationship). Establishes everything needed to follow the song. Example: Prince "Purple Rain," The Beatles "Yesterday," Dotson's "Everybody Wants to Go to Heaven."
    - **Chorus:** The point of the song, big idea/emotion. Contains the title. No new characters/storylines. Examples: The Eagles "Life in the Fast Lane," Michael Jackson "Man in the Mirror."
    - **Verse 2:** Takes the big idea/emotion further. Answers "Then what?". Must advance the song, not repeat V1 or introduce new characters/context. Crucial to avoid "song killer" second verses.
    - **Bridge (Middle Eight):** Brief musical contrast. Takes idea further, sums up, or introduces a twist. Advances the song. Optional (Kim Williams quote: "Don't build a bridge if there's no river to cross"). Ideal song length (2:45-3:00) often precludes bridges. Dotson estimates <25% of his songs have bridges.
    - **Outro:** Musical end. Usually no new lyrics, may repeat last chorus line.
- **Complete Song Blueprinting Example:** Lyrics for "Must Be Doing Something Right" (Billy Currington) are provided, noting its lack of a bridge.
- **Song Length:** Emphasizes keeping songs short, editing out non-essentials.
- **Exercise:** Guides the reader to create their first song blueprint (V1, Chorus, V2).
- **Keys to Blueprinting:** Simple, clear, direct. One idea per section (avoid commas/ands). Consistent voice.

**Chapter 4: Song Furniture (Elevating Your Song Lyrics with Details, Texture, and Spice)**

- **Core Principle:** "Show, Don't Tell." Use specific details to let listeners draw their own conclusions.
- **Analogy:** Vague song = empty room. Furniture = details for listener to relate to.
- **"Landing the Plane":** Moving from general (30,000 ft view) to specific sensory details (sight, hear, touch, smell, taste). Imagine a video of the song.
    - Example: "I know you're still mad" (telling) vs. "You turn your back when we lie in bed at night" (showing).
    - Vivid examples from "The Highway Don't Care" and "I Drive Your Truck."
- **Elements of Furniture (with numerous song title examples for each):**
    - **Locations:** Specific place names (cities, states, businesses).
    - **People's/Animals' Names:** Adds specificity.
    - **Times/Dates/Days/Seasons/Parts of Day:** Grounds the song.
    - **Colors:** Adds visual appeal.
    - **Weather:** Creates atmosphere.
    - **Objects:** Concrete items.
    - **Body Parts:** Relatable and often emotive.
    - **Numbers:** Adds specificity.
    - **Brand Names:** Adds realism (generally no permission needed if used positively).
    - **Specific Actions/Descriptive Verbs:** More engaging than generic verbs (e.g., "tangled" vs. "involved").
    - **Age:** Specific and relatable.
    - **Dialogue:** Brings scenes to life (examples: "Ode to Billy Joe," "The Chair").
    - **Noun Modifiers:** Makes nouns more interesting/specific (e.g., "deep blue need you eyes," "last call kissing").
    - **Phrases Turned into Nouns:** Creative way to add texture (e.g., "cups of who cares," "get my forget you on").
- **Moving Furniture/Furniture Warning:** Use the right amount; too much is cluttered, too little is bare.
- **Summary of Furniture Categories:** Sensory words, props, names, numbers, times, dialogue, locations, juicy verbs, modifying adjectives, phrases as nouns.

**Chapter 2: Ideas and Hooks** *(Appears here in the transcript, out of numerical sequence)*

- **Central Idea:** Song should be about one clear, focused "big idea" (big enough for many to care about).
- **Title/Hook:** The entry point, should be memorable.
    - Hook definition: Listener can sing title/hook after a few listens (Johnny Reed "Fire It Up" example).
    - Placement: Often starts/ends chorus.
- **Generating Ideas, Hooks, and Titles:**
    - **Quotation Sites:** Google "love quotes" (images), reellifewisdom.com (movie dialogue).
    - **Random Pages/Words:** From books/magazines (Gary Burr "In Front of the Alamo" example).
    - **Book Titles:** Titles not copyrightable, but avoid very famous ones. (e.g., "Love is a Verb," "All the Pretty Girls").
    - **Inversions/Variations of Well-Known Phrases:** (e.g., "Friends in Low Places," "The House That Built Me").
    - **Lines from Movies/TV Shows:** (e.g., "Blue Clear Sky" from Forrest Gump, "You Had Me at Hello").
    - **Read Lyrics by Top Songwriters:** For inspiration (not theft).
    - **"Rhyme Lines" Book:** (Out of print) for expressions, phrases.
    - **Things People Say:** Eavesdropping (Rivers Rutherford "Real Good Man" example).
    - **Objects:** Look around for inspiration (Toby Keith "Red Solo Cup").
- **Universal Emotions as Song Themes:** Love, unrequited love, meaning of life, cheating, loss, good times, nostalgia, escape.
- **Make Your Song Idea Universal:** Balance personal details with universal appeal.
    - Dotson's "She Stopped Living the Day He Died" example – too personal, publisher rejected.
    - Questions to ask: Who cares? Fresh take? Universal enough?
- **Song Framing:** Finding a unique slant/angle on a universal emotion.
    - Examples: Dirk Bentley "Black" (seduction, not depression), Little Big Town "Girl Crush" (envy of new girl).
- **Brainstorming Unique Angles:**
    - **Associations:** List words/phrases related to title/idea (e.g., "Body Like a Back Road," "Hotel California," "train" example).
    - **Analogies & Similes:** Comparing one thing to another (e.g., "Love is Like Rain," "Love is a Rose").
    - **Opposites/Oxymorons:** (e.g., "The Truth About Lies," "Beautiful Mess," "Live Like You Were Dying").
    - **Multiple Meanings:** Using words/phrases with several interpretations (e.g., "Lie, Cheat, or Steal," "Doing It to Country Songs," "Dirt").
    - **Thinking Outside the Box:** No stupid ideas in brainstorming; explore weird angles.
    - **Find the Truth in Your Idea:** Connects with audience (e.g., "Everybody Wants to Go to Heaven" – truth is people don't want to die *now*).

**Chapter 5: Digging deeper into song blueprinting methods**

- Starts with Abraham Lincoln quote ("Give me six hours to chop down a tree...").
- **Simplicity:** One sentence per section for clarity, as listeners are often distracted.
- **Balance in Blueprinting:** Avoid being too specific (limits flexibility) or too vague (unhelpful).
- **"No ifs, ands, or buts":** Each section summary should be one simple idea.
- **Example: "Let Me Down Easy" (Billy Currington)**
    - Blueprint: V1 (I'm feeling something strong for you), C (If I fall in love, please let me down easy), V2 (I'm so taken by you that you fill my senses).
    - The blueprint defines *what* to communicate, allowing flexibility in *how* (setting, storyline).
- **Using Summary Phrase in Lyrics:** Sometimes the blueprint sentence can be a lyric itself (e.g., "Everybody Wants to Go to Heaven" chorus).
- **Crucial Concept:** A good blueprint *opens up* possibilities; a weak one *limits* them. Contrasts an open vs. a limiting blueprint for "Let Me Down Easy."
- **Story Songs as Exceptions:** Sometimes a specific story dictates a more locked-in blueprint.

**Chapter 6: Writing to the Title (One Thing)**

- **"City Slickers" Analogy:** Focus on "one thing." A song should have one clear message.
    - Test: Summarize song in one short phrase. Ask others to do so.
- **Writing *to* Your Title (vs. *from*):** Every section and line should point towards and set up the title/hook (usually in the chorus).
    - Publisher's exercise: Read a line, then the title; repeat for every line.
    - Examples: Bonnie Raitt "I Can't Make You Love Me," Lee Brice "I Drive Your Truck."
- **Title/Hook Placement:** Typically first or last line of chorus, or both. Can be title-only choruses ("Born in the USA") or refrains ("Tangled Up in Blue").
- **Why not write *from* title:** Can lead to meandering. Writing *to* provides direction.
- **Ultimate Goal for Commercial Song:** Earworm, memorable, audience sings along. Repetitive and memorable choruses achieve this (e.g., "Fire It Up," "Doing It to Country Songs").
- **Starting Point:** Envision title in the center; all other parts are circles pointing to it. "The song is king" – discard great lines if they don't support the title.

**Chapter 7: Finishing Touches. Lyric Editing.**

- "Good songs are written, great songs are rewritten." Review after a week or two.
- **Lyric Pitfalls & Fixes:**
    - **Inconsistent Rhyme Schemes (within sections):** Maintain pattern (AABB, ABAB etc.) within verses, though sections (verse vs. chorus) can differ.
    - **Changing Pronouns/Points of View:** Stick to one (1st, 2nd, or 3rd person) unless using dialogue.
    - **Unclear Pronouns:** Ensure clarity if multiple characters of the same gender.
    - **Inconsistent Verb Tenses:** Stick to one (present, past, future), usually present for immediacy.
    - **Time Frame/Scene Jumps:** Avoid jarring, unexplained jumps.
    - **Twisting Lines for Rhyme ("Yoda Speak"):** Prioritize natural, conversational language over forced rhymes. Test: "Would you say this in real life?"
    - **Songwriter's Assumptions:** Don't assume listeners know what you know; provide necessary context. (Ralph Murphy anecdote).
    - **Second Verse-itis (Fatal Song Disease):**
        - Repeating V1 in different words.
        - "Chasing rabbits" (irrelevant content).
        - Contradicting the hook.
        - Confusing time movement.
        - "Dropping the ball" (weaker than V1/Chorus). Tip: Swap V1 and V2, write new V1.
- **Checklist for Editing (11 points):**
    1. Message clear? (Communicate first, then be clever. Example: "I Can't Make You Love Me").
    2. Pronouns consistent & clear?
    3. Wrote *to* the hook?
    4. Anything work *against* the hook?
    5. Extra/useless words?
    6. Lines hard to sing?
    7. Timeline make sense?
    8. How song portrays singer (sympathetic)?
    9. Lyric believable/natural?
    10. Stayed focused on one idea (hook)?
    11. Would I pay money for this song?
- **Ignoring "Rules" vs. Communication Rules:** Focus on clear communication. Breaking communication norms (like switching POV mid-song) is confusing, not just a broken "songwriting rule." (Chuck Jones anecdote about repeating words).
- **Exercise:** Read lyric aloud like a letter to catch errors.
- **"Kill Your Darlings" (Faulkner):** Be willing to cut great lines if they don't serve the song. "The song is king."

**Chapter 8: Using song blueprinting in co-writing sessions.**

- **Co-writing Prevalence:** Common in Nashville, LA, Stockholm, NY. Collaboration can elevate writing.
- **Challenge:** Aligning all writers. Dotson's anecdote of a 4-hour co-write producing two different songs.
- **Blueprinting as Alignment:** Ensures everyone is writing the same song, saving time and frustration. Even informal blueprinting (discussing section goals) helps.
- **Co-writing with Recording Artists:**
    - Time is often limited.
    - Ask what the artist wants to say; get their buy-in on a blueprint.
    - Research artist beforehand (interviews, past work).
    - Key questions for artists (what song are you missing? what do you want to say?).
    - Artist's ideas are "gold."
- **Bringing Out the Best in Co-writers:**
    - Goal: Help co-writers shine. Aim for "exponential" co-writes (result > sum of talents).
    - Ask how they work best (title first, groove, melody? slow/fast? detail/big picture?).
    - Adapt your style; give them space. Become a "super collaborator."
- **Dealing in Real / Your Unique Experience:**
    - Avoid formulas or being "too creative" beyond real life.
    - Your unique life experience is your "secret weapon."
    - Exercise: Write 5 statements you'd like to say to someone; brainstorm song titles. Leads to authentic, deep songs.
- **Making Your Story Everyone's Story:**
    - Use personal experience as a foundation but generalize for broader appeal.
    - Revisits his "She Stopped Living the Day He Died" (too personal) vs. "Why You Loved Me" (universal version of similar emotion).
- **Trust Yourself:** Imitate heroes, learn craft, then develop your unique style.

**Send Off: Write Better Songs**

- The answer to "How do I get cuts/publishing deal/better co-writers?" is "Write better songs."
- Blueprinting, clear communication, writing from the heart are key.
- **10,000-Hour Theory:** Mastery comes from practice and growth. Your 1000th song will be far better than your 1st or 100th.
